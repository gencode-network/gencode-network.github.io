The advent of increasingly accessible and affordable genomic technology has provided new approaches to study community assemblages. A recent surge in community genetics and genomics research utilizes this technology to understand how assemblage patterns and hybridization associate with genomic change across co-occurring taxa. These methods bring us closer to addressing some of the core challenges of the scope and variability of community ecology, and allow us to study dynamic systems lacking long-term historical data.

We can now use previously inaccessible data from modern and historic genomic surveys to model community assemblage and dynamics over time, and predict system response to climatic stress – all essential tools to determine system resilience and target conservation efforts. However, definitions of and approaches to community genomics can vary across research communities and siloed subdisciplines. To harness the immense potential of community genomics in service to conservation, we seek to understand the current state of community genomics research, synthesize broad and concrete definitions of related terminology, and build connections across taxa, ecosystems, and disciplines to provide a clear path forward for the advancement of this emergent field.
